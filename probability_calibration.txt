> Our XGBoost model outputs a probability for each day.
    > Something like "72% chance NY continues London today"
        > Does that actually mean anything?
            > Calibration asks: on all the days the model said 70%, did continuation actually happen 70% on the time.

> Why it matters in trading specifically:
    > In most ML applications we just care whether the prediction is right or wrong.
        > In trading we care about the probability estimate itself because it directly feeds into position sizing
            > Kelly Criterion.

> Two types of miscalibration:
    > Overconfident - the model probabilities are too extreme, clustering near 0 & 1.
    > Underconfident - probabilities cluster too close to 0.5, and the model is too hesitant.

> The reliability diagram:
    > The main visual output is the reliability diagram.
        > predicted probability on the x-axis, actual frequency on the y-axis.
        > Perfect calibration would be a diagonal line.
        > Points above the diagonal mean Underconfident.

> How to fix poor calibration:
    > Sklearn has two calibration methods we can apply on top of the existing model:
        > Platt scaling - fits a logistic regression on top of the model outputs. Better for small datasets
        > Isotonic regression - fits a flexible monotonic function. 
            > Better for larger datasets but can overfit with small samples.
    > Platt probably better for our situation but we try both.
