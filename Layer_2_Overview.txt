The Goal:
    > Shifting from describing what happened to predicting what will happen.
        > Build a classifier that takes session features as input and predicts a binary outcome
            > e.g. whether NY will continue or reverse london's direction.

Step 1 - Feature Engineering:
    > Transform raw session data into predictive features
        >e.g.:
            - Asia range as a percentile of its own 20-day rolling history (normalised, so comparable across pairs and time)
            - London range / Asia range ratio
            - Whether London opened above or below Asia's midpoint
            - Asia and London direction (bullish/bearish)
            - Day of the week (Monday sessions behave differently to Friday)
            - London Range as a percentile of its 20-day history

Step 2 - Define target variable:
    > Binary outcome we are trying to predict (e.g. 1 = NY continues London direction, 0 = NY reverses London)

Step 3 - Logistic Regression:
    > Fast and good baseline for further analysis.
    > Coeffs tell us directly which featurs are pushing the prediction in each direction

Step 4 - XGBoost/LightGBM:
    > Captures nonlinear relationships that logistic regression misses (how?)
        > Logistic regression draws a straight decision boundary through feature space, learns a weight for each feature and combines them.
            > Linear.
        > XGBoost & LightGBM are <Gradient boosted decision tree> models. 
            > Rather than linear, they build hundred of small decision trees where each learns errors from prior:
                Is Asia range small?
                    YES → Is London bullish?
                            YES → predict continuation (70%)
                            NO  → predict reversal (60%)
                    NO  → Is London range large?
                            YES → predict continuation (55%)
                            NO  → predict reversal (52%)
    > More powerful but less interpretable, so we add SHAP values on top

Step 5 - Time series cross-validation:
    > Use walk-forward approach where you train on the past and test on the immediate future, rolling forward in windows

Step 6 - SHAP Values:
    > Explains which features drove each individual prediction.
    > Clear visual overview and answers "why did the model predict this"

Step 7 - Probability calibration:
    > Checks whether our model's predicted probabilities are trustworthy
        > If the model says 70% confidence does it actually win 70% of the time?
