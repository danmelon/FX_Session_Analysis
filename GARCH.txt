> What are GARCH models:
    > Standard statistics assumes volatility is constant over time.
        > We know this is not true in markets.
        > Markets have volatility clustering.
    
    > GARCH (Generalised Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity) captures this by modelling today's volatility as a function of:
        > Yesterday's volatility estimate
        > Yesterday's actual return shock
            > The result is a daily conditional volatility estimate - how volatile the model thinks today will be based on recent history.

> How we use it for session analysis:
    > Once we have a vol estimate for each day, we split the data into regimes.
        > High vol regime - top 33% of vol days
        > Medium vol regime - middle 33%
        > Low vol regime - bottom 33%

    > The we re-run our session break probabilities, continuation rates and XGBoost accuracy within each regime seperately.
        > Then:
            > Does London break Asia more often in high volatility regimes?
            > Is NY continuation stronger or weaker when volatility is elevated?
            > Does the V shape in asia_mid_to_london_close persist across all regimes or only specific ones?

> What to expect:
    > High volatility regimes will almost certainly show higher session break rates.
        > This is mechanical since larger moves are more likely to exceed prior session highs and lows.
            > The more interesting finding would be if the directional continuation rateor the V shape changes meaningfully across regimes.
                > Would suggest the relationship between sessions is regime-dependent.
